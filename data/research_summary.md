# Quantum Computing Hardware: Recent Advancements (Past Three Months)

## Hardware Breakthroughs and New Systems

- **Rigetti’s Multi-Chip Fidelity Breakthrough:** Rigetti Computing announced it achieved a **99.5% median two-qubit gate fidelity** on a new 36-qubit **modular** quantum processor – a **two-fold error rate reduction** compared to its previous 84-qubit single-chip system. The 36-qubit device is composed of **four 9-qubit “chiplets”** tiled together (a first in the industry), demonstrating a path to scale beyond a single chip. Rigetti plans to **launch this 36-qubit system in August 2025** and remains on track to deliver a **100+ qubit chiplet-based system** (also targeting ~99.5% fidelity) by the end of 2025.

- **Oxford Ionics **Quartet** System Deployment:** UK-based startup **Oxford Ionics** delivered and installed a **full-stack trapped-ion quantum computer** called **QUARTET** at the UK National Quantum Computing Centre (NQCC) in Harwell in August 2025【53:6†source】. QUARTET leverages Oxford Ionics’ proprietary **Electronic Qubit Control** technology – using chip-based electronic controls instead of lasers – which enables **all components for trapping and controlling ions to be integrated on a standard semiconductor chip**【53:6†source】. This approach has yielded record-high fidelity in quantum operations, and the system is **field-upgradeable** by swapping out its credit-card-sized quantum processor unit, allowing easy upgrades to higher qubit counts and performance in the future【53:6†source】.

- **Japan’s First Domestic Quantum Computer:** In late July 2025, Japan launched its **first fully homegrown superconducting quantum computer**, which went live at Osaka University’s Center for Quantum Information and Quantum Biology (QIQB). This machine was **designed and built entirely with domestic Japanese components and software**, showcasing Japan’s technological prowess and marking a significant milestone for its national quantum program. The  superconducting quantum processor’s successful deployment demonstrates Japan’s capacity to develop large-scale quantum hardware independently, a point celebrated as the system began operation on July 28, 2025.

## Roadmaps for Next-Generation Quantum Hardware

- **IBM’s Fault-Tolerant Quantum Blueprint:** IBM unveiled a **roadmap to build the world’s first large-scale, fault-tolerant quantum computer** by 2029, code-named **IBM Quantum “Starling.”** Announced in June 2025, this plan aims for a system with **200 logical (error-corrected) qubits** capable of executing 100 million quantum operations. The effort relies on a new architecture with advanced error-correcting codes (qLDPC) and a series of **modular superconducting processors** – **Loon** (expected 2025), **Kookaburra** (2026), and **Cockatoo** (2027) – that will be networked together. IBM’s Starling, to be built in a new quantum data center in New York, is projected to perform **20,000× more operations than today’s quantum machines** and serve as the foundation for a future **“Blue Jay”** system with 2,000 logical qubits (targeted by 2033),. IBM’s CEO highlighted this as “the next frontier” in quantum, emphasizing that achieving fault tolerance at scale could unlock practical solutions in drug discovery, materials science, and optimization.

- **IonQ’s Accelerated Scaling Roadmap:** Trapped-ion quantum computing company **IonQ** announced an **accelerated hardware roadmap** in June 2025, outlining an ambitious leap in qubit count over the next few years. By leveraging two recent acquisitions – **Oxford Ionics** (for chip-integrated ion traps) and **Lightmatter’s** LightSYNQ division (photonic interconnects) – IonQ plans to boost its systems from today’s tens of qubits to **~20,000 physical qubits by 2028** and about **2,000,000 qubits by 2030**【53:1†source】, . Crucially, thanks to error correction, ~20,000 physical qubits in 2028 would yield roughly **1,600 stable logical qubits**, and the 2030 goal would correspond to tens of thousands of logical qubits【53:1†source】. IonQ likened its 2028 vision to a “**distributed quantum supercomputer**” comprised of two entangled ion-trap chips linked by photonic networks【53:1†source】. To reach these targets, IonQ is integrating Oxford Ionics’ high-fidelity ion-trap-on-chip technology with its own systems. The $1.075 billion acquisition of Oxford Ionics (announced June 2025) underscores this strategy – the combined company expects to build systems with **256 qubits at 99.99% fidelity by 2026**, over **10,000 qubits (with 99.99999% fidelity) by 2027**, and ultimately **2 million qubits by 2030**, hitting the scale needed for full fault tolerance , . IonQ’s CEO remarked that this roadmap, accelerated by the merger, “**accelerates our mission to [build] 2 million physical qubits and 80,000 logical qubits by 2030**” – positioning IonQ at the forefront of the quantum hardware race .

## Industry Partnerships and Collaborations

- **IBM & TCS – Quantum Hub in India:** In May 2025, IBM and Tata Consultancy Services (TCS) announced a partnership with the government of Andhra Pradesh to deploy **India’s largest quantum computer** as part of a new “Quantum Valley Tech Park” in Amaravati. The tech park will be anchored by an **IBM Quantum System Two** installation featuring IBM’s latest **156-qubit “Heron” superconducting processor**, which would make it the most powerful quantum computer in India. TCS will collaborate with IBM on developing quantum algorithms and applications to spur India’s quantum ecosystem, and cloud-based access to IBM’s existing quantum machines will be provided to local researchers in the interim,. This initiative aligns with India’s National Quantum Mission to establish the country as a **global quantum technology hub**, and represents a major public-private effort to advance quantum computing capabilities in the region.

- **Microsoft & Atom Computing – “Level 2” Quantum System (QuNorth):** **Microsoft** and startup **Atom Computing** are teaming up to build one of the world’s first **“Level 2” quantum computers** – a system that can perform computations using *logical qubits* (error-corrected qubits). In July 2025, Denmark’s Export and Investment Fund (EIFO) and the Novo Nordisk Foundation announced an **€80 million investment** to establish a new Nordic quantum initiative called **QuNorth**, which will **acquire a cutting-edge quantum computer nicknamed “Magne.”**. **Magne** will be built jointly by Atom Computing (providing its neutral-atom based hardware) and Microsoft (contributing advanced quantum control software), and is expected to be operational in Copenhagen by early 2027,【53:7†source】. According to Microsoft’s CEO Satya Nadella, this project represents the **world’s first operational deployment of a Level 2 quantum computer**, signaling a critical step toward practical fault-tolerant quantum computing【53:7†source】. The Magne system aims to offer Nordic researchers and industries priority access to a powerful quantum machine for applications in chemistry, materials science and beyond, helping the region catch up in the global quantum race.

- **IonQ & Emergence – Expanding Quantum Hardware R&D in Australia:** In July 2025, IonQ announced a **strategic collaboration with Australia-based Emergence Quantum** to co-develop **next-generation ion-trap hardware**, expanding IonQ’s footprint in the Asia-Pacific region. The partnership will focus on improving trapped-ion quantum processors by jointly developing advanced **electronics, materials, and application-specific integrated circuits (ASICs)** for qubit control. Emergence Quantum’s team, composed of former Microsoft and Raytheon quantum hardware leaders, brings deep expertise in cryogenic electronics and systems engineering to the effort. IonQ stated the goal is to **boost ion trap performance, simplify hardware complexity, and scale up system capacity** through this collaboration. This move also taps into Australia’s growing quantum talent pool and reinforces IonQ’s commitment to global partnerships in pushing quantum hardware forward. *Related:* Earlier in July, IonQ was **selected as the primary tech provider for South Korea’s new National Quantum Computing Center**, where it will deliver a **100-qubit quantum system** and co-develop a hybrid quantum-classical platform for that national program. Together, these initiatives show IonQ forging international alliances to accelerate hardware innovation and deployment.

- **D-Wave & Yonsei University – Quantum Annealer in South Korea:** **D-Wave Quantum Inc.** (a leader in quantum annealing systems) announced in June 2025 a memorandum of understanding (MOU) with **Yonsei University** and the City of Incheon in South Korea to establish a joint quantum technology initiative. As part of this strategic relationship, the parties plan to **install a D‑Wave **Advantage2™** quantum annealer at Yonsei University’s Songdo campus**, marking one of the first on-site deployments of D-Wave’s latest model in Asia. The partnership will focus on *research collaboration, workforce development,* and developing practical use cases in areas like biotechnology and materials science using D-Wave’s quantum technology. Yonsei’s president noted that introducing D-Wave’s annealing quantum computer on campus will create significant opportunities for scientific breakthroughs and industry applications in Korea. For D-Wave, which operates both annealing and gate-model quantum computers, this deal furthers its mission to **accelerate global adoption** of quantum computing by establishing South Korea’s Incheon region as a new **quantum innovation hub**,.

## Major Funding Announcements in Quantum Hardware

- **Record Funding Surge in Q1 2025:** The quantum computing sector saw a significant influx of capital early in 2025, indicating rising investor confidence in hardware progress. In Q1 2025 alone, quantum computing companies raised over **$1.25 billion** – more than double the amount from the year prior【53:3†source】. Notably, several hardware-focused firms closed **mega-rounds**: **IonQ** secured approximately **$360 million**, **QuEra Computing** (neutral-atom quantum computers) raised **$230 million**, and **Quantum Machines** (quantum control hardware) raised **$170 million** in a Series C round【53:3†source】. This wave of funding – making up over 70% of all quantum-tech investments for the quarter – underscores a pivotal shift toward scaling up **quantum processors and infrastructure** for commercial readiness【53:3†source】,【53:3†source】.

- **Rigetti’s $350 Million Capital Raise:** In June 2025, superconducting quantum hardware firm **Rigetti Computing** completed an at-the-market equity offering, **raising $350 million** in gross proceeds【53:2†source】. This sizeable cash infusion brought Rigetti’s total liquidity to roughly **$575 million (with no debt)**, providing a strong runway to support the **scale-up of its quantum processor roadmap**【53:2†source】. Rigetti indicated it will use the funds for working capital, expanding its Fab-1 chip manufacturing facility, and potentially strategic partnerships or acquisitions【53:2†source】. Coming shortly before Rigetti’s multi-chip technology milestone, this funding positions the company well to pursue its goal of delivering a 100+ qubit high-fidelity system by year-end.

- **Infleqtion (ColdQuanta) $100 Million Series C:** **Infleqtion** – a U.S. startup specializing in **atom-based quantum systems** (formerly known as ColdQuanta) – announced a **$100 million Series C** funding round in early June 2025【53:8†source】. Backed by investors including Glynn Capital, Morgan Stanley’s Counterpoint Global, and aerospace/defense contractor SAIC, this investment will help Infleqtion **scale up its neutral-atom quantum computing platforms and accelerate deployment of field-ready quantum hardware**【53:8†source】. Infleqtion had about $30 million in revenue last year and a pipeline of $200 million in customer projects, and it plans to use the new capital to expand production of its quantum processors and related quantum sensing technologies【53:8†source】. Notably, Infleqtion has systems already deployed internationally (its **“Squale”** quantum computer is installed at the UK’s NQCC) and is working with partners in the U.S. and Japan on national defense and research applications【53:8†source】. The Series C round also came with a strategic partnership: **SAIC will co-market Infleqtion’s quantum solutions to government and defense customers**, reflecting how quantum hardware companies are aligning with industry allies as they scale【53:8†source】.

- **QuantWare’s €27 Million Round for Quantum Chips:** **QuantWare**, a Dutch quantum hardware startup, raised a total of **€27 million (≈\$27M) in Series A funding** by mid-June 2025 to boost its production of superconducting quantum processors. The Delft-based company develops **scalable quantum processor units (QPUs)** and has proprietary technology (called “VIO”) aimed at enabling million-qubit scale quantum chips through modular integration. This oversubscribed round – backed by European deep-tech funds including the EU’s EIC fund and regional investors – will finance the expansion of QuantWare’s fabrication facilities and hiring of top engineering talent. QuantWare’s business model of providing **off-the-shelf and custom QPUs** (with an open architecture approach) has attracted interest as it allows other organizations to build quantum computers without developing their own chips, a sign of the maturing supply chain in quantum hardware.

- **Additional Notable Investments:** The broader quantum hardware ecosystem has seen several other significant investments in recent months. For example, **Boulder Ionics** (quantum hardware components) raised seed funding to advance novel cryogenic amplifiers【53:4†source】, and **Keysight Technologies** delivered a state-of-the-art **quantum control system** to Japan’s AIST, reflecting investments not only in qubits but also in the surrounding infrastructure【53:5†source】. These trends – alongside national funding initiatives (such as Japan’s ¥6 billion program for domestic quantum projects, and the EU’s ongoing Quantum Flagship grants) – indicate that both **private capital and government support** are fueling rapid progress in quantum computing hardware as the field transitions from laboratory research into scaled-up industry solutions.
